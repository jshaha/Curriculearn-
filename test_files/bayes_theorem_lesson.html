Why Bayes' Theorem Matters

Imagine you take a medical test that claims to be "95% accurate." You test positive. Should you panic?

The answer might surprise you - it depends on how rare the disease is! This is where Bayes' Theorem becomes crucial.

Real-World Applications:
  • Medical diagnosis and testing
  • Spam email filtering
  • Criminal trial evidence evaluation
  • Machine learning algorithms
  • Weather forecasting updates
Slide 3

The Big Idea: Updating Beliefs

Bayes' Theorem is fundamentally about updating what you believe as you get new information.

Simple Example:

You hear footsteps in your house at night.

  • Initial belief: It's probably my cat (90% sure)
  • New evidence: The footsteps are heavy and slow
  • Updated belief: It's probably a person, not my cat (new probability)

Bayes' Theorem gives us a mathematical way to do this updating formally.

Slide 4

Quick Review: Conditional Probability

Before we get to Bayes' Theorem, let's review conditional probability:

P(A|B) = "Probability of A given that B happened"
Example:
  • P(Rain | Dark Clouds) = Probability it rains, given there are dark clouds
  • P(Flu | Fever) = Probability you have flu, given you have a fever

The "|" symbol means "given" - it tells us we have new information to consider.

Slide 5

The Formula

P(A|B) = [P(B|A) × P(A)] / P(B)

Where:

  • P(A|B) = Posterior probability - what we want to find (probability of A given evidence B)
  • P(B|A) = Likelihood - probability of seeing evidence B if A is true
  • P(A) = Prior probability - our initial belief about A before seeing evidence
  • P(B) = Marginal probability - total probability of seeing evidence B

The formula can also be expanded:

P(A|B) = [P(B|A) × P(A)] / [P(B|A) × P(A) + P(B|¬A) × P(¬A)]

Where ¬A means "not A" and represents all cases where A doesn't happen.

Odds Form: O(A|B) = O(A) × [P(B|A) / P(B|¬A)]
Slide 6

Mathematical Derivation

Bayes' Theorem can be derived from the definition of conditional probability:

P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B)

And:

P(B|A) = P(A ∩ B) / P(A)

From the second equation:

P(A ∩ B) = P(B|A) × P(A)

Substituting into the first equation:

P(A|B) = [P(B|A) × P(A)] / P(B)

Therefore, we've proven Bayes' Theorem from first principles.

Example 1: Medical Testing

Scenario:
  • A disease affects 1% of the population
  • A test for the disease is 95% accurate (both for positives and negatives)
  • You test positive. What's the probability you actually have the disease?

Let's define our events:

  • A = You have the disease
  • B = You test positive

What we know:

  • P(A) = 0.01 (1% have disease)
  • P(B|A) = 0.95 (95% of sick people test positive)
  • P(B|¬A) = 0.05 (5% of healthy people test positive - false positive rate)
Slide 8

Solution: Step by Step

Step 1: Calculate P(B) using the law of total probability:

P(B) = P(B|A) × P(A) + P(B|¬A) × P(¬A)
P(B) = (0.95 × 0.01) + (0.05 × 0.99) = 0.0095 + 0.0495 = 0.059

Step 2: Apply Bayes' Theorem:

P(A|B) = [P(B|A) × P(A)] / P(B)
P(A|B) = (0.95 × 0.01) / 0.059 = 0.0095 / 0.059 ≈ 0.161

Answer: Only about 16% chance you actually have the disease!

Slide 9

Why Is It Only 16%?

This surprising result happens because the disease is rare.

Imagine testing 10,000 people:
  • 100 actually have the disease (1%)
  • Of those 100, about 95 test positive (true positives)
  • 9,900 are healthy
  • Of those 9,900, about 495 test positive anyway (false positives)

Total positives: 95 + 495 = 590 people

Actually sick: 95 people

Probability: 95/590 ≈ 16%

Most positive tests are false positives because the disease is so rare!

Slide 10

Example 2: Spam Filtering

Scenario:

An email contains the word "FREE". What's the probability it's spam?

  • 40% of all emails are spam
  • 70% of spam emails contain "FREE"
  • 5% of legitimate emails contain "FREE"

Define events:

  • S = Email is spam
  • F = Email contains "FREE"

Apply Bayes:

P(S|F) = [P(F|S) × P(S)] / P(F)
Slide 11

Your Turn: Solve the Spam Problem

Given:
  • P(S) = 0.40
  • P(F|S) = 0.70
  • P(F|¬S) = 0.05

Find P(S|F)

Work it out before moving to the next slide!

Slide 12

Solution: Spam Example

Step 1: Calculate P(F):

P(F) = P(F|S) × P(S) + P(F|¬S) × P(¬S)
P(F) = (0.70 × 0.40) + (0.05 × 0.60) = 0.28 + 0.03 = 0.31

Step 2: Apply Bayes:

P(S|F) = (0.70 × 0.40) / 0.31 = 0.28 / 0.31 ≈ 0.903

Answer: About 90% chance it's spam!

The word "FREE" is strong evidence for spam because it appears much more often in spam (70%) than in legitimate emails (5%).

Slide 13

Key Takeaways

Remember:
  • Bayes' Theorem helps us update probabilities with new evidence
  • Rare events can make test results surprising (medical test example)
  • The formula: P(A|B) = [P(B|A) × P(A)] / P(B)
  • Always consider base rates (how common something is initially)
Common Mistake: Ignoring the base rate! Even highly accurate tests can be misleading when testing for rare conditions.
